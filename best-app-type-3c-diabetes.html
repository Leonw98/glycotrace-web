Why Type 3c diabetes is harder to track than other types

If you have Type 3c diabetes, you already know the frustration. Your blood sugar after breakfast on Tuesday looks nothing like the same breakfast on Friday. You are managing insulin and enzyme replacement, often with very little guidance on how they interact. And when you search for a tracking app, every result points you to something built for Type 1 or Type 2 users.

That disconnect is not a coincidence. Type 3c is caused by damage to the pancreas rather than autoimmune destruction or insulin resistance, which means the underlying biology is genuinely different. Your exocrine pancreas is also affected, so digestion itself is unpredictable. When absorption varies from one meal to the next depending on how well your enzyme replacement worked, your glucose response will vary too, regardless of how carefully you counted the carbs.

Standard tracking apps are not built for this. They treat every carb gram as if it will be absorbed at the same rate. They have no concept of enzyme replacement. They cannot help you correlate Creon timing with a post-meal spike two hours later.

What mainstream apps get wrong

MySugr is probably the most recommended diabetes app and it is genuinely good for what it does. The logging interface is clean, it connects to some glucometers, and the summary reports are useful for clinic appointments. But it was designed around Type 1 management. There is no PERT field, no way to log enzyme replacement, and no mechanism to link a dose of Creon to what happened to your glucose an hour later. For Type 3c, that missing link is the whole problem.

Glooko is popular in clinical settings because it can pull data from a wide range of CGM and pump devices. It is excellent for sharing data with a diabetes team. But again, the assumption baked into the product is that carb absorption is predictable. For someone with exocrine insufficiency, that assumption fails constantly.

LibreView (Abbott's own platform) shows your sensor glucose data clearly and the time-in-range reporting is genuinely useful. But it is a CGM viewer, not a full logging tool. You cannot log meals, insulin, or PERT doses alongside your glucose readings.

None of these apps are bad. They just were not built for you.

What to look for in a Type 3c tracking app

Before downloading anything, it is worth knowing what actually matters for Type 3c management:

  • Meal logging with carbs and fat separately. Fat slows gastric emptying, which affects when glucose rises. Logging both helps you spot patterns that carb-only logging misses.
  • Medication and PERT logging. You need to be able to record when you took enzyme replacement relative to your meal, not just that you took it.
  • Insulin logging. Both basal and bolus, with timestamps, so you can see how your corrections worked.
  • CGM integration. Manual finger-prick logging is fine but if you use a Libre sensor, automatic import saves time and gives you a more complete picture.
  • Correlations over time. A single day's data tells you very little. The value is in seeing patterns across weeks: which meal combinations produce spikes, when hypos tend to happen, how pancreatitis episodes affect your baseline.
